Description

For the Summer of 2018, I served as a UX Developer for a web platform called QUINCE, an abbreviation for the lengthy title: Quality of Internet Consumer Experience. This platform enables end-users to easily participate in various internet measurement tasks. Participants can, in turn, accumulate virtual points and redeem them for rewards in the form of currency.

Responsibility

Under the supervision of Ricky, I helped design and develop the web platform to increase participant engagement. Naturally, I spent some time researching effective ways to gamify measurement tasks so that they would appeal more to paricipants. As for the visual side, I worked directly to improve the layout of pages, enhance visualizations by trying out various icons and images, and implement interactive elements. I also had to repair visual bugs and design breakdowns for the existing platform. And last but not least, I was tasked with visualizing the participants’ measurement results.

The consent form is the first thing participants see when accessing the web platform. The form was expanded vertically to cover a larger area of the screen than before and a shadow was placed at the bottom of the form to indicate that there is scroll functionality. At a glance, the consent form is an organized, interactive element that informs participants.

The tutorial modal window introduces participants to the interface and what their jobs are. Originally, the window was much larger to accomodate a bulkier logo and the oversized font. The redesign lessened font size and inserted helpful paragraph spacing. The logo was replaced by a more appropriate sized one and the Continue button was adjusted to sit flush with the edges of the modal window.

I designed and implemented the onboarding process that includes the sign-in, sign-up, and forgot password screens. I also conceptualized this design with the other modal windows in mind. I chose a deep green to symbolize the currency that participants may earn through the web platform. Lastly, I employed adequate negative spacing and contrast.

In order to implement a subtle yet immersive, interactive US map, I dug into amCharts, a JavaScript charting and mapping library, to tweak changes within the code. The result is a seamless experience between interacting with the map and visualizing geographical locations. Notice, there is also a home button for those who get carried away.

Conclusion + Takeaways

Starting off at the internship was a little rough: I had to become familiar with Meteor, get certain dependencies installed on my computer, join services for communication with the team, attain access for special privileges, etc.

However, good things take time and effort. And so, not suprisingly, progressing through the project and absorbing knowledge from everyone made it all worth the hassle. Working closely with researchers in CAIDA and getting valuable experience working with the collected datasets was a privilege. Working in teams definitely helped advance my design and interpersonal skills because of the opportunities to receive feedback on my work ranging from mere conceptual ideas to high-fidelity quality.

Thank you to Ricky for the mentorship and guidance and to Jennifer for the awesome collaboration.
